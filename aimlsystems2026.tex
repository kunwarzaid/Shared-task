\title{Trustworthiness of LLMs in Cross-Lingual Clinical Summarization:
An Empirical Study on Indian Languages}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

% ==================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Clinical AI systems are overwhelmingly benchmarked in English, yet over
a billion-plus speakers of Indian languages rely on healthcare systems
where consultations occur in diverse regional languages. We study whether
current open-source LLMs can generate trustworthy English clinical
summaries directly from multilingual doctor--patient dialogues, a
practically important setting that has received almost no systematic
attention. We evaluate two 7B-parameter LLMs (Mistral-7B and Qwen2.5-7B)
across ten typologically diverse Indian languages under four settings:
zero-shot prompting, few-shot prompting ($k{=}1$), translation of source
dialogues to English prior to summarization, and LoRA-based supervised
fine-tuning.

We find that few-shot prompting unexpectedly \emph{degrades} performance
in 8 of 10 language pairs; translation helps Indo-Aryan languages but harms
Dravidian languages, revealing a strong typological divide; and clinical
safety remains a major concern, with scores consistently below 3.0/5.0
across all conditions. Supervised fine-tuning substantially improves
performance, eliminating cross-lingual disparities and reducing
hallucination rates from $\sim$42\% to $\sim$10\%. We additionally conduct
systematic qualitative case analyses across all ten languages, providing an
eight-category clinical error taxonomy that characterizes failure modes by
method and language family.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Accurate and timely documentation of clinical encounters is a cornerstone
of modern healthcare systems. Clinical consultations between doctors and
patients typically contain a rich mix of essential details --- including
symptoms, diagnostic reasoning, medication histories, treatment decisions,
and relevant psycho-social context --- all of which are vital for accurate
documentation and continuity of care, yet much of this content is never
reliably captured in structured clinical records. In India alone, primary
healthcare workers conduct hundreds of millions of consultations annually
across a population that speaks 22 constitutionally scheduled languages
spanning four major language families~\cite{gala2023indictrans2}. The
cognitive burden of manual documentation in this setting is substantial,
and errors of omission in clinical notes are well-documented as a leading
source of adverse patient outcomes~\cite{clusmann2023future}.

Automatic summarization of clinical dialogues using Large Language Models
(LLMs) offers a promising path toward reducing this burden. Recent work has
demonstrated that instruction-tuned LLMs achieve competitive performance on
English clinical summarization benchmarks~\cite{tang2023gersteinlab,
abacha2023overview}. However, the overwhelming majority of clinical NLP
research targets high-resource languages --- most often English --- leaving
a vast multilingual clinical documentation need unaddressed. Real-world
clinical communication spans diverse languages, and healthcare delivery
frequently occurs in multilingual and low-resource linguistic environments.
For Indian languages, which range from relatively well-resourced Indo-Aryan
languages such as Hindi and Bengali to morphologically complex languages
like Marathi, and script-distinct Dravidian languages such as Telugu, Tamil,
and Kannada, existing LLM-based summarization pipelines are entirely
unevaluated.

In this paper, we conduct a systematic investigation of the trustworthiness
of LLM-based clinical summarization across \textbf{ten typologically diverse
Indian languages} as shown in Table~\ref{tab:language_data}. The languages
span five script families, two major typological groupings (Indo-Aryan and
Dravidian), and a spectrum of NLP resource availability from high (English,
Hindi) to severely low (Dogri, Assamese). We analyze the trustworthiness of
cross-lingual clinical summarization systems, focusing on truthfulness,
safety, and explainability. We examine how linguistic and resource
differences influence summarization quality and demonstrate that performance
degrades substantially for low-resource languages. To the best of our
knowledge, this is the first systematic study that characterizes
trustworthiness failures in multilingual clinical dialogue summarization for
Indian languages. Our principal contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item We present the first comprehensive evaluation of open-source LLMs
  on clinical dialogue summarization across ten typologically diverse Indian
  languages, spanning multiple scripts, language families (Indo-Aryan and
  Dravidian), and resource levels.

  \item We propose a multi-dimensional evaluation framework that integrates
  traditional lexical metrics, LLM-as-a-judge scoring for trustworthiness
  aspects (truthfulness, safety, explainability), and clinical entity-level
  analysis.

  \item We benchmark four widely used strategies: zero-shot prompting,
  few-shot prompting, translate-then-summarize, and supervised fine-tuning,
  providing a unified comparison under a consistent experimental setup.

  \item We demonstrate a pronounced performance disparity across languages,
  revealing significant gaps not only in overall summarization quality but
  also in key trustworthiness attributes between high-resource and
  low-resource settings.

  \item We contribute an eight-category \textbf{Clinical Error Taxonomy}
  characterizing recurring failure modes by paradigm and language family,
  grounded in qualitative case analyses across all ten languages.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Automatic summarization of doctor--patient conversations has emerged as a
central task in clinical NLP over the past decade. Early work applied
extractive and abstractive sequence-to-sequence models to English medical
conversations, with systems achieving competitive performance on structured
clinical note generation~\cite{joshi2020dr,abacha2023empirical}. The
MEDIQA-Chat 2023 shared task~\cite{abacha2023overview} provided a
large-scale evaluation benchmark, catalyzing systems that fine-tuned
pre-trained dialogue summarization models and employed in-context learning
with GPT-3 and GPT-4. \citeauthor{tang2023gersteinlab}~\cite{tang2023gersteinlab}
demonstrated that fine-tuned GPT-3 and in-context learning in GPT-4 achieve
strong ROUGE-1 and BERTScore on the Dialogue2Note subtask, while
\citeauthor{mathur2023summqa}~\cite{mathur2023summqa} found that GPT-4
generated summaries tend to be shorter and more abstractive than
human-authored notes, highlighting a systematic coverage gap that we
replicate and quantify in our multilingual setting.

\citeauthor{vanveen2023adapted}~\cite{vanveen2023adapted} demonstrated that
domain-adapted LLMs can outperform medical experts on English clinical text
summarization benchmarks. \citeauthor{FraileNavarro2025}~\cite{FraileNavarro2025}
conducted expert evaluations of multiple LLMs on clinical dialogue
summarization, and \citeauthor{Ferreira2025}~\cite{Ferreira2025} extended
this evaluation to real-world non-English data (Portuguese), finding that
open-source LLMs struggle significantly with informal, noisy patient-provider
dialogues --- a challenge directly amplified in our setting by the added
dimension of script diversity and low resource availability. None of these
works address cross-lingual clinical summarization for Indian languages.
\citeauthor{zeng2020meddialog}~\cite{zeng2020meddialog} introduced one of
the first datasets for medical conversation summarization covering English
and Chinese. \citeauthor{lyu2025automatic}~\cite{lyu2025automatic} show
the performance of clinical summarization using prompt tuning on a domain
specific LLM. Clinical NLP in multilingual settings has primarily focused
on entity recognition and concept normalization rather than
summarization~\cite{gaschi2023multilingual}.
\citeauthor{abrar2025faithful}~\cite{abrar2025faithful} proposed a
cross-lingual framework for faithful summarization of consumer health
queries using LLMs combining TextRank extraction, medical NER, and
fine-tuned LLMs.

\citeauthor{gala2023indictrans2}~\cite{gala2023indictrans2} introduced
IndicTrans2, the first neural machine translation system supporting all 22
scheduled Indian languages, trained on 230 million sentence pairs, and
substantially outperforming prior systems across language pairs relevant to
this work. For clinical NLP specifically, multilingual resources for Indian
languages are scarce. \citeauthor{singh2025indihealthbench}~\cite{singh2025indihealthbench}
evaluated LLMs for clinical translation across Indian languages, finding
that performance degrades sharply for Dravidian and low-resource Indo-Aryan
languages, consistent with the zero-shot performance gaps we report across
entity-level metrics. \citeauthor{ghosh2025clinic}~\cite{ghosh2025clinic}
evaluated multilingual trustworthiness in healthcare LLMs, demonstrating
pronounced safety and factuality drops across low-resource languages. While
prior benchmarks focus on medical QA or translation, we extend evaluation
specifically to \emph{cross-lingual clinical dialogue summarization}.

\section{Task, Data, and Models}
\label{Task}

\subsection{Task Definition}

We study the problem of cross-lingual clinical dialogue summarization,
where the input is a multi-turn doctor--patient conversation in an Indian
language and the output is a structured clinical summary in English,
matching realistic deployment contexts where medical records are maintained
in English while consultations occur in the patient's native language.

\subsection{Dataset}

We use data from the Shared Task on Patient-Centric Clinical Dialogue
Summarization.\footnote{\url{https://www.codabench.org/competitions/10527/}}
The corpus comprises train/validation/test splits of carefully annotated
clinical encounters. The dataset contains 231 dialogues across the ten
languages (approximately 20--25 per language), with each dialogue paired
with a gold English summary. The dialogues exhibit high linguistic
complexity: lengths vary dramatically, ranging from 4,000 to over 7,000
tokens per encounter, and natural code-switching with English medical
terminology is highly prevalent across all regional languages. We use 50
examples from the training data as the test set for evaluation.
Table~\ref{tab:language_data} describes the language properties.

\subsection{Models}

\textbf{Mistral-7B-Instruct}~\cite{jiang2023mistral} uses grouped query
attention and sliding window attention for efficient inference. Its
pretraining is primarily European-centric, which explains its relatively
weaker zero-shot performance on non-Latin-script languages.

\textbf{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct}~\cite{qwen25techreport} was trained on 18T
tokens with explicit multilingual coverage, including Indic scripts.

Both models fit within a single 32\,GB GPU, making them practical for
privacy-constrained clinical deployment.

\section{Methodology}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.90\linewidth]{Images/final_example.pdf}

    \label{fig:example}

\noindent\textbf{Zero-shot (ZS).}
The model is directly prompted to generate a structured clinical summary
from the input dialogue without any task-specific examples. This serves as
the primary baseline and reflects minimal deployment overhead.

\noindent\textbf{Few-Shot (FS) / In-Context Learning.}
Few-shot prompting augments the zero-shot prompt with a small number of
demonstration examples, $k{=}1$ in our case, drawn from the training set.
During preliminary experiments, we attempted $k{>}1$; however, because
raw clinical dialogues are exceptionally long, providing multiple examples
exceeded our 32\,GB VRAM constraint. Each example consists of a
doctor--patient dialogue and its corresponding gold English summary. The
objective is to condition the model on the desired output format and
clinical content structure, thereby reducing the ambiguity inherent in
open-ended summarization tasks.

\noindent\textbf{Translation (Trans).}
A well-established strategy for low-resource NLP is translate-then-summarize:
translate the source text into a high-resource language, then apply the NLP
pipeline. Given the strong English summarization capabilities of both target
models, we explore whether routing non-English dialogues through a
high-quality neural machine translation (NMT) system before summarization
yields improvements over direct multilingual inference. Non-English source
dialogues are first translated to English using
IndicTrans2~\cite{gala2023indictrans2}, then summarized under the same
zero-shot English prompt. The hypothesis is that routing through English
allows models to summarize in their strongest generation language,
compensating for weaker multilingual generation calibration.

\noindent\textbf{Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).}
We fine-tune both Mistral-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct using Low-Rank
Adaptation (LoRA) on the full multilingual training set. For Qwen2.5-7B, we
employ rank $r{=}8$, $\alpha{=}32$, and dropout $0.05$, targeting the
\texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, \texttt{o\_proj},
\texttt{gate\_proj}, \texttt{up\_proj}, and \texttt{down\_proj} layers. Both
models are trained using 4-bit NF4 quantization, for 3 epochs using AdamW
with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and an effective batch size of 8
(per-device batch size 1 with gradient accumulation of 8 steps). We enable
gradient checkpointing to optimize memory.

\subsection{Evaluation Framework}

We employ a three-tier evaluation framework assessing generated clinical
summaries from complementary perspectives.

\textit{Lexical and semantic similarity metrics}: We use
ROUGE-L~\cite{lin2004rouge} and BERTScore~\cite{zhang2019bertscore} to
evaluate summarization quality. Both are necessary but insufficient: a model
can score well on surface overlap while hallucinating drug names or omitting
diagnoses. We include them for comparability with prior work.

\textit{LLM-as-judge}: We use GPT-4o-mini to score each summary on three
dimensions using structured rubrics on a 1--5 Likert scale (where 3 is
``acceptable'' and 5 is ``excellent'').
\textbf{Truthfulness}: Are all claims medically accurate and grounded in the
conversation?
\textbf{Safety}: Does the summary avoid omissions, distortions, or
fabrications that could lead to harmful clinical decisions?
\textbf{Explainability}: Can a clinical reader trace each substantive claim
back to a specific point in the dialogue, with correct negation, temporality,
and speaker attribution?
The judge receives the source dialogue, the generated summary, and the gold
reference (for orientation only, not as ground truth). To validate this
automated evaluation, we conducted a human study across four languages
(Tamil, Kannada, Marathi, Hindi) using the same three-dimensional rubric
plus an overall trust rating; results are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:human_eval}.

\textit{Clinical entity analysis}: We use GPT-4o-mini to extract five entity
categories --- Symptoms, Diagnoses, Substances and Habits, Procedures and
Tests, Treatments --- from both generated summaries and source dialogues,
normalizing morphological variants to canonical forms. An entity from the
summary is a true positive (TP) if semantically equivalent to a dialogue
entity; a false positive (FP, \emph{hallucination}) if it appears in the
generated summary but not the dialogue; a false negative (FN,
\emph{omission}) if a dialogue entity is absent from the generated summary.
We report Precision, Recall, F1, Hallucination Rate
($\text{FP}/(\text{TP}+\text{FP})$), and Omission Rate
($\text{FN}/(\text{TP}+\text{FN})$). We do not use fuzzy string matching
for entity evaluation as it falsely penalizes valid clinical synonyms.

\subsection{When In-Context Learning Fails}
\label{sec:fewshot}

adding same-language demonstrations makes things worse, not better, across
most conditions. Macro-average entity F1 falls from 0.518 to 0.499 for
Qwen and from 0.432 to 0.385 for Mistral. Hallucination rates rise by 3.4
and 4.6 percentage points. Table~\ref{tab:macro_metrics} shows the full
picture of zero-shot versus few-shot deltas.

We identify three mechanisms. First, \textit{template lock}: the
demonstration summary encodes a particular clinical narrative structure,
and the model adheres to it even when the test dialogue contains content
that falls outside that structure. Omission rates increase in 14 of 20
language--model combinations under few-shot. Second, \textit{entity bleed}:
entities from the demonstration dialogues --- patient demographics,
medication names, symptom descriptions --- migrate into the generated
summary for a different patient. This inflates hallucination counts with
content that is clinically real but belongs to the wrong case, making the
error invisible to surface-level metrics. Third, \textit{calibration
disruption}: for models already well-calibrated to clinical summarization
at zero-shot, the demonstration introduces a competing prior that
interferes with generation. The English zero-shot condition illustrates
this clearly: Mistral-English F1 drops from 0.652 to 0.558 under few-shot,
the largest single degradation in the Mistral few-shot conditions, and
precisely where the model is best calibrated.

These observations are consistent with prior work showing that
demonstrations primarily encode format rather than semantic
content~\cite{min2022rethinking}, and that few-shot biases can actively
hurt performance compared to zero-shot when the model's prior is already
reasonable~\cite{zhao2021calibrate}. There are two exceptions:
Qwen-Gujarati (F1 $+0.134$) and Mistral-Telugu (F1 $+0.096$). Both have
the highest zero-shot hallucination rates in their respective matrices
(0.693 and 0.718): few-shot is beneficial only when zero-shot generation
is poor; otherwise it introduces more noise than signal.

One observation worth noting is that ROUGE-L and BERTScore are nearly
insensitive to the zero-shot/few-shot difference ($\Delta < 0.012$), while
entity-level metrics show meaningful changes. This is a concrete reminder
that surface overlap metrics should not be the primary evaluation criterion
in clinical settings, where the cost of a fabricated drug name or missed
diagnosis far exceeds the cost of slightly different phrasing.

For Dravidian languages, the story is very different. Qwen-Telugu drops

Kannada and Tamil show similar degradation. The issue is that IndicTrans2

failure is not in the summarizer --- it is in the translation step. This

from the summary silently.

Bengali specifically, translation hallucination nearly doubles (0.375 to
0.676), suggesting translation quality is lower than expected given the
language's resource level. This is a useful caution against assuming that
script family predicts translation quality; language-specific validation is
necessary before deployment.

macro-averaged 2.217 (Qwen) and 2.197 (Mistral), with Mistral-Assamese
reaching 1.42/5.0. For Dravidian languages and low-resource Indo-Aryan
languages, direct zero-shot inference is safer than translation.
Practitioners should not assume that routing through a high-resource
language uniformly improves summary quality.

performance gap that was real and sizable under prompting is essentially
eliminated by fine-tuning. \emph{The gap is not inherent to the
languages --- it is an artifact of insufficient task-specific training.}
This has direct implications for equity in clinical AI deployment.

The hallucination reductions are equally striking. Macro-average
hallucination drops from 42.4\% to 10.5\% for Qwen and from 50.9\%
to 9.8\% for Mistral. Mistral-Telugu, which had a hallucination rate
of 71.8\% at zero-shot, falls to 7.0\% post fine-tuning --- below the
English zero-shot rate of 29.6\%. After fine-tuning, every language for
both models achieves a hallucination rate below 14.5\%.

Safety scores improve from around 2.5 (zero-shot) to 2.96 (Qwen SFT)
and 2.92 (Mistral SFT). Importantly, neither model crosses 3.0/5.0 as a
macro-average even after fine-tuning. The gap between Truthfulness and
Safety scores \emph{widens} under fine-tuning: at zero-shot it is
$\approx$0.76 (Qwen) and 0.60 (Mistral); at SFT it grows to 1.19 and
1.17. Models become more truthful much faster than they become safer.
This dissociation occurs because Safety penalizes not just factual errors
but also actionable omissions, inverted negations, incorrect dosages, and
incomplete management plans --- a richer set of clinical harms that
supervised imitation of gold summaries does not directly optimize for.
Closing this gap likely requires safety-specific training signals, such
as rubric-grounded reward models or reinforcement learning from clinical
expert feedback.

\subsection{Qwen vs.\ Mistral: A Comparative Analysis}

At zero-shot, Qwen2.5-7B leads Mistral-7B on every entity-level metric:
F1 0.518 vs.\ 0.432 ($+$0.086), precision 0.576 vs.\ 0.490, recall 0.484
vs.\ 0.392, hallucination 0.424 vs.\ 0.510. Qwen also leads on LLM-judge
dimensions: Truthfulness by 0.226 and Explainability by 0.336. The one
exception is ROUGE-L, where Mistral is marginally higher (0.1352 vs.\
0.1318), but this reflects shorter, more formulaic outputs that happen to
share surface n-grams with gold summaries --- not better clinical coverage,
as confirmed by lower entity F1 and BERTScore. Qwen's advantage traces to
its broader multilingual pretraining: token representations for non-Latin
scripts are better calibrated, giving stronger generative confidence in
Dravidian and low-resource Indo-Aryan languages.

After fine-tuning, the gap narrows substantially: 0.775 vs.\ 0.760 F1
($+$0.014). Practitioners working in resource-constrained settings should
prefer Qwen2.5-7B for prompting-based deployment; for fine-tuned
deployment, both models are viable.

Safety is the lowest-scoring dimension in every single experimental
condition for both models, with the cross-lingual macro-average failing
to reach 3.0/5.0 even post-SFT. The scores range from 1.42
(Mistral-Assamese, translation) to 3.08 (Qwen-Kannada, SFT).

severe safety collapses. After SFT, the language-family safety gap narrows
but does not disappear entirely.

\section{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:human_eval}

To validate our automated LLM-as-a-judge evaluation, we conducted a human
study across four languages: Tamil, Kannada, Marathi, and Hindi. For each
language, annotators evaluated zero-shot and few-shot summaries using the
same three-dimensional rubric as our LLM judge --- Truthfulness, Safety,
and Explainability --- plus an overall trust rating. The human annotations
corroborate our automated findings: while few-shot prompting often improves
the superficial flow and formatting of the summary (reflected in higher
Explainability ratings), it actively introduces severe clinical
hallucinations (e.g., fabricating geographic locations such as ``Pune''
not present in the source dialogue) and degrades factual Truthfulness.
This consistent agreement between human annotators and GPT-4o-mini
judgments validates the reliability of our automated evaluation framework
for safety-critical clinical dimensions across language families.

\section{Discussion}

Our results expose a concrete, quantified reliability gap in multilingual
clinical AI. Dravidian language users face hallucination rates roughly
15 percentage points higher than English users at zero-shot. Translation
makes this worse, not better. Few-shot prompting also degrades performance
across the board. Fine-tuning on domain-matched multilingual data is the
only strategy that closes the gap. Deploying English-optimized clinical
LLMs without fine-tuning produces documentation that is measurably less
safe for Dravidian-language speakers than for English speakers.

The few-shot finding deserves specific attention: same-language
demonstrations still hurt performance, ruling out cross-lingual
interference as the sole explanation. The entity-bleed mechanism we
identify is specific to domain-homogeneous settings where demonstration
and test dialogues share a clinical context: entities transfer plausibly
but incorrectly. This is a warning for any high-stakes domain where
few-shot prompting uses in-domain examples.

The persistent safety deficit after SFT points to a training objective
gap: imitating gold summaries optimizes truthfulness but does not penalize
dangerous omissions, inverted negations, or collapsed conditional plans.
Safety-specific reward models or learning from clinical expert feedback
are likely necessary. For the AI-ML systems community, this highlights a
concrete deployment engineering challenge: clinical summarization pipelines
need language-stratified evaluation infrastructure, translation quality
gating before the summarization stage, and safety-aware reward modeling as
a first-class component rather than an afterthought.

% ==================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We present the first systematic evaluation of cross-lingual clinical
dialogue summarization across ten Indian languages. Few-shot prompting
consistently degrades performance through template lock, entity bleed, and
calibration disruption. Translation through IndicTrans2 helps Indo-Aryan
languages but harms Dravidian and low-resource languages through
mistranslation cascades. Clinical Safety scores remain below 3.0/5.0
across all conditions and both models, even after fine-tuning. Supervised
fine-tuning closes the cross-lingual performance gap: Kannada and Telugu
reach F1 scores competitive with English for entity coverage. Safety
evaluations conducted only in English systematically underestimate the
clinical risk faced by speakers of low-resource languages, and evaluation
frameworks must reflect this reality.
